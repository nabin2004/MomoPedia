## Overview

Tibet is where the momo story begins. High on the Tibetan Plateau — the "Roof of the World" — nomadic herders and traders adapted Chinese dumplings using yak meat, highland barley, and the limited vegetables available at extreme altitudes. The result was a hearty, robust dumpling designed to fuel life in one of Earth's harshest environments.

## The Tibetan Style

Tibetan momos differ from their Nepali and Indian cousins in several key ways:

| Feature | Tibetan Style |
|---|---|
| **Size** | Large — 4-inch diameter, a plate of 8 is a full meal |
| **Dough** | Thicker, chewier, made with highland barley or wheat flour |
| **Filling** | Yak meat (or mutton), minimal spices — meat flavour dominates |
| **Cooking** | Steamed in metal steamers, sometimes boiled in broth |
| **Sauce** | Simple soy-chilli dip or chilli oil, not complex achari |

## Cultural Significance

- **Losar (Tibetan New Year):** Families make hundreds of momos together. Hidden inside some: chilli (sharp tongue), coin (wealth), or wool (soft heart) as playful fortunes.
- **Monastic food:** Vegetable momos are common in Buddhist monasteries, where many monks are vegetarian.
- **Community bonding:** Momo-making gatherings (*momo parties*) are a social institution.

## Regional Variations

- **Lhasa:** The classic — large, yak-filled, served with soy-chilli dip on Barkhor Street.
- **Amdo (Northeast Tibet):** Influence from Chinese Muslim cuisine; mutton momos with cumin.
- **Kham (Eastern Tibet):** Cheese-filled momos using dried yak cheese (*chhurpi*).
- **Dharamshala (India, Tibetan exile):** Adapted to Indian ingredients while preserving traditional techniques.
