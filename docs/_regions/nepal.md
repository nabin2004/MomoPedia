## Overview

Nepal is arguably where the momo reached its fullest cultural expression. Introduced by Newari traders who encountered Tibetan dumplings along trans-Himalayan trade routes, momos were adopted, adapted, and elevated into Nepal's unofficial national dish. Today, you cannot walk 100 metres in Kathmandu without encountering a momo stall.

## The Varieties

Nepal boasts the widest range of momo styles in the world:

| Style | Description |
|---|---|
| **Steamed (Classic)** | The original — soft pleated dumplings in a bamboo steamer |
| **Kothey** | Pan-fried on the bottom, steamed on top — crispy and juicy |
| **Jhol** | Steamed momos floating in a spicy sesame-tomato broth |
| **C-Momo** | Open-topped dumplings drizzled with spiced butter |
| **Fried** | Deep-fried to a golden crunch, often with chaat masala |
| **Chilli Momo** | Stir-fried with bell peppers and a sweet-spicy sauce |

## Regional Differences

- **Kathmandu Valley:** The most diverse — all styles available. Newari-influenced fillings often include buff (water buffalo).
- **Pokhara:** Lakeside restaurants cater to tourists with fusion momos (cheese, chocolate, pizza momo).
- **Eastern Nepal (Ilam, Dharan):** Stronger Tibetan influence, larger momos, yak or pork fillings.
- **Terai (Southern plains):** Vegetable momos dominate, often spicier, with Indian-influenced chutneys.

## Nepali Momo Culture

Momos are deeply woven into Nepali social life:

- **Date food:** "Momo khana jaam?" ("Let's go eat momos?") is a common way to ask someone out.
- **Festival staple:** Large batches are made during Dashain, Tihar, and family gatherings.
- **Economic engine:** The momo industry employs tens of thousands of Nepalis, from street vendors to restaurant chains.

> Nepal consumes an estimated **20 million momos per day** across its population of 30 million.
