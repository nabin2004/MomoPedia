## Origin Story

Jhol momo (literally "soup momo") was born in the lanes of Kathmandu in the early 2000s when enterprising street vendors began serving steamed momos drowned in a hot, tangy broth of tomatoes, sesame seeds, Szechuan pepper (*timur*), and dried chillies. The dish was an instant hit: it gave you the beloved momo *and* a warming soup in one plate, perfect for chilly Himalayan evenings.

While the exact inventor is debated — at least three Kathmandu shops claim the title — the consensus is that jhol momo evolved organically from the long tradition of pairing momos with *jhol achar* (liquid chutney). Someone simply poured more achar around the momos, and a new genre was born.

## History & Cultural Significance

Jhol momo represents the innovative spirit of Nepali street-food culture. Within a decade of its invention, it spread from Kathmandu to every major Nepali city and into the Indian cities of Darjeeling, Siliguri, and Delhi. It is now one of the most ordered items on Nepali food-delivery apps.

During Nepal's *bandh* (strike) days — when streets emptied and shops closed — the neighbourhood momo stall serving jhol momo often stayed open, becoming a gathering point for communities waiting out the shutdown.

> **Fun Fact:** Some restaurants serve "dry jhol momo" — which is technically a contradiction ("dry soup momo") — where the broth is reduced to a thick, clinging sauce.

## What Makes It Special

- **The Broth:** A complex blend of roasted sesame, tomato, *timur* (Szechuan pepper), fenugreek, and dried chilli creates a soup that is tangy, nutty, and tingling simultaneously.
- **Temperature Contrast:** Warm broth keeps the momos hot longer, unlike bare steamed momos that cool quickly.
- **Customisable Heat:** Vendors adjust chilli levels per order — from mild to "Nepali hot."

## Jhol (Broth) Recipe

| Ingredient | Quantity |
|---|---|
| Tomatoes, chopped | 3 medium |
| Sesame seeds, roasted | 2 tbsp |
| Dried red chillies | 3–5 |
| Szechuan pepper (*timur*) | 1 tsp |
| Fenugreek seeds | ¼ tsp |
| Garlic | 4 cloves |
| Turmeric | ¼ tsp |
| Water | 2 cups |
| Salt, sugar, lemon juice | to taste |

## Preparation

1. Dry-roast sesame seeds, *timur*, fenugreek, and dried chillies until fragrant.
2. Blend roasted spices with tomatoes, garlic, and turmeric into a smooth paste.
3. Simmer paste with water for 10 minutes, adjusting salt, sugar, and lemon juice.
4. Steam momos separately using the classic method.
5. Ladle hot jhol broth into bowls, nestle 6–8 momos in each, and serve immediately.

## Interesting Tidbits

- Jhol momo is often called Nepal's dumpling soup, but it's technically "chutney with dumplings" — the ratio of momo-to-broth is much higher than a typical soup.
- The *timur* (Szechuan pepper) creates a numbing-tingling sensation unique to Himalayan cuisine, distinct from the burn of chilli heat.
- In Bhaktapur, a variation uses fermented bamboo shoot (*tama*) broth, adding a sour, funky dimension.
