## Origin Story

Kothey momos emerged in the bustling streets of Kathmandu's Ason and Indra Chowk bazaars, where vendors needed a way to rescue unsold steamed momos from the previous batch. The solution? Slap them on a hot oiled griddle. The result was a dumpling with a golden-crispy bottom and a soft steamed top — a textural masterpiece born from frugality.

The name *kothey* comes from the Newari word meaning "pan-fried" or "stuck to the pan." Unlike Japanese gyoza — which are often compared — kothey momos are thicker-skinned and more generously filled, staying true to Himalayan dumpling proportions.

## History & Cultural Significance

Kothey momos gained mainstream popularity in the 1990s as Kathmandu's street-food scene exploded. Vendors realised that the sizzling sound and golden crust attracted more customers than steamed momos alone. Today, most momo shops in Nepal offer kothey as a standard option alongside steamed and fried.

In the Newari community, kothey momos are often served during *bhoj* (communal feasts) as a starter, paired with beaten rice (*chiura*) and spicy achar.

> **Fun Fact:** Some Kathmandu shops double-cook kothey momos — steaming them first, then griddling — for maximum juiciness inside with an extra-crispy shell.

## What Makes It Special

- **Dual Texture:** Crispy caramelised bottom meets pillowy steamed top.
- **Flavour Boost:** The Maillard reaction on the bottom adds a nutty, savoury depth absent in steamed versions.
- **Presentation:** Served bottom-up to show off the golden crust — a point of pride for vendors.

## Preparation

1. Prepare dough and filling as for classic steamed momos.
2. Shape momos with a flat bottom (half-moon or round).
3. Heat oil on a flat pan/griddle over medium-high heat.
4. Place momos flat-side down. Cook for 2–3 minutes until golden.
5. Add ¼ cup water and cover immediately. Steam-fry for 8–10 minutes.
6. Remove lid, let remaining water evaporate, and serve crispy-side up.

## Interesting Tidbits

- In Pokhara, some shops add cheese inside kothey momos — a fusion twist that tourists love.
- Kothey momos are the preferred style for late-night snacking in Thamel, Kathmandu's tourist district.
- The technique is nearly identical to Chinese *guotie* (pot-stickers), suggesting shared culinary ancestry along the Silk Road.
